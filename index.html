Here's how recent book bans reveal the erosion of democracy

Book bans signal a larger problem for the nation: proportional representation in policymaking, elections, and educational decisions.

By Cameron Samuels Dec. 5, 2023

Book bans are rising at record pace in America, and Texas has led the nation in pulling books from school shelves. While challenges to library books and curriculum are regular occurrences for school officials, this unprecedented wave of censorship has brought upon challenges to 2,571 unique titles in 2022 alone. This movement to censor remains strong this year, and it's not merely about the books. Recent censorship, especially in states like Texas, is a symptom of a larger problem for the nation: the erosion of an educated, strong democracy. Understanding the underlying roots of book bans requires looking back to pivotal moments of opposition to the Civil Rights Movement with a movement to privatize schools in Texas.

In the Texas educational landscape, the concept of school vouchers can be traced as far back as 1957 with a neo-Confederate attempt to pass a bill offering grants to students withdrawing or refusing to attend schools that integrated after Brown v. Board (1954). The bill states it “will provide a means for fair choice of school” and clarifies that “each person in this State has the right to avoid forced integration.” Seeking to reinforce financial power dynamics, the legislation relieved families with state funding that would “otherwise be paid to the district of residence.”

“School choice” is a clever term. Nearly every legislative session since 1999, Texas lawmakers proposed school voucher bills – including three failed attempts this year. The proposal is to essentially establish a state-sponsored tuition assistance program for K-12 private or religious schooling, which are not required to follow state achievement standards or nondiscrimination protections. Many stakeholders, including educators, students, and families, fear vouchers would only exacerbate inequality in a system already divided by wealth and status whilst not granting much of a choice to marginalized students.

Vouchers have taken shape in various proposals and have gone by many names over the years, from “voucher pilot program for certain children” to “taxpayer savings grant” and “education savings account” in most recent attempts. In fact, lawmakers have come to strictly avoid using the word “vouchers” in policy language to fend off criticism and reframe the proposal, notably since Rep. Richard Raymond began filing legislation for a constitutional ballot initiative to prohibit state funding for school vouchers.

Legislative attempts to pass vouchers in Texas, by session year State lawmakers in Texas have proposed some form of voucher legislation nearly every session over the past few decades. While failed attempts and bipartisan opposition deterred attempts in 2019 and 2021, an unprecedented record of at least 15 voucher bills were proposed in 2023 amid “parental empowerment” rhetoric by the state's top officials. (Source: Texas Legislature Online)

When the 88th Texas Legislature convened in January, lawmakers stridently brought “parental empowerment” to the forefront unlike ever before. Many candidates ran on campaign platforms promising to center parents in educational decisions affecting their children – a significant nationwide trend since the COVID-19 pandemic. Gov. Greg Abbott has even played a major role in bolstering this call at rallies and campaign events this year, along with asserting what he can of his official influence on the state's legislative agenda.

Despite this year's reckoning, bringing a record high number of individual proposals for school vouchers, all 15 bills in the regular session met similar fates, once again. In decades of state history, Texas lawmakers have, in pity to voucher proponents, only prevailed in amending the state's biennial budget to prohibit usage of funds for vouchers in the fiscal period.

Retrospectively, the choice to revive this effort in present-day debates over educational power and opportunity, time and time again, has been firmly made by almost identically the same population as when proposed in 1957, only generations younger. The mostly-White and commonly-wealthy voucher proponents clearly understand their intentions and its lucrative outcome as a handshake deal. Beneficiaries receive financial assistance for their children's elite education, not out of their pocket but at the expense of state funding to neighborhood public schools, where around 96% of the state's students attend, mostly students of color.

The sociopolitical “school choice” movement's racial undertones are undeniable. History and data alike unequivocally show that vouchers only serve to widen existing inequalities in education. Instead of uplifting all students, they empower a select few, leaving the vast majority – predominantly students of color – struggling in underfunded neighborhood schools. Once explicitly aimed at perpetuating segregation, the interchangeable “parental empowerment” movement is a chapter of Texas history that remains powerful and is still influencing education.

On the censorship front, book bans in recent years have swooped into play as a red herring, seeking to draw controversy to both long-standing and progressive social concepts in schools. Critical Race Theory and “gender ideology” content have become the new scapegoat for an America where politics calls the shots in the classroom. Misled by conservatism and motivated by ignorance, no respect for deeper learning or dismantling oppressive power structures is held. Rather, there's interest for the contrary.

In October 2021, near the onset of this uproar, Texas Rep. Matt Krause launched an investigation into school libraries. Krause delivered a letter requesting school districts to examine library collections for a list of books and report findings to the Texas House Committee on General Investigating. Of roughly 850 titles included in his extensive investigation, most were books that touched upon themes of race, gender, and sexuality.

In the two months following Krause's letter, Texas began its rampage over school libraries; school districts pulled from shelves a total of 632 titles. As with the FBI's foundation upon the Palmer Raids and Sen. Joseph McCarthy's House Committee on Un-American Activities, the movement to censor is a concerted political effort led by few who hold great influence in what mainstream America considers acceptable to the status quo.

Cleansing the literary canon, by whitewashing history curriculum and vilifying LGBTQ+ identity in schools, is a core tactic for accomplishing the goals of a fringe but powerful minority in Texas. And there's a very specific reason for this challenge taking center stage in education, the foundation of the future of Texas.

In the legacy of Engel v. Vitale (1962), which ruled official prayer unconstitutional in public education, a core sect of America was particularly unhappy. At the demise of racial segregation and Christian prayer in public schools, many Evangelical Christians look to this moment in history when tracing back what they consider to be America's moral downfall.

"Since it was the schools that put teaching Christian values and morals, I believe that was kind of the beginning of the decline," said Linda Kellogg, Texas grandmother and founder of the National Motto Project. "The culture and education system was not blessed by God anymore. Education seems to be what has prompted a lot of the radical beliefs we are seeing erupt."

Politicians and interest groups like the National Motto Project have been influential since 2021 in passing state legislation mandating the placement of “In God We Trust'' signs in public schools and allowing school districts to replace school counselors with religious chaplains.

"When the national motto bill got stuck a time or two, I asked my prayer teams to pray," Kellogg said. "I believe that God was helping us. I believe that prayer really helped."

Since signed into law and implemented across Texas, the bill's author shamelessly clarified the bill's ambiguous text after local activists cleverly tested its integrity. While the bill technically requires school districts to accept applicable donations, Carroll ISD refused when Sravan Krishna, father of two in the district, presented iconic frames of “In God We Trust” in Arabic and rainbow typography at a board meeting in August 2022. Krishna, whose family is Hindu, was frustrated with attempts to push the "Christian nationalist worldview" into his children's public education.

"These are all signs of their Christian nationalism being brought into or perpetrated in our schools," Krishna said. "I don't necessarily like going to the board room, yet on this particular occasion, I went there because I was a volunteer representing the community members. It is a very uncomfortable, very vile kind of atmosphere. It was one of those things I had to do."

"Why is more God not good? This is also our national motto,” Krishna said during his public remarks at the board meeting after the board president cited, as reason for declining to accept the donation, that all schools already posted applicable “In God We Trust” signs.

While the state legislature has historically avoided asserting power over local decision-making, this is not the only social policy imposed upon school districts by the legislature in recent years. When perusing legislative colleagues who sponsor bills like Senate Bill 797, the national motto bill, close similarities are conspicuous alongside book ban bills like House Bill 900, curriculum gag orders like S.B. 3, university bans on inclusion efforts like S.B. 17, and teen Internet censorship like H.B. 18.

This self-proclaimed legislative attempt to “take back our schools” is framed ostensibly with a “parental rights” mantra but has given way for increased state authority over local governance. Furthermore, the statewide movement is far from organic, driven by conservative enterprises like “America's only Christian conservative wireless provider,” Patriot Mobile, and faith-based media empires like WallBuilders, which teaches widely discredited pseudohistory and is led by ill-famed David Barton. From each of their perspectives, there's a common but deceptive understanding that today's America is not what it used to be nor what it should be.

“WallBuilders is an organization dedicated to presenting America's forgotten history and heroes, with an emphasis on the moral, religious, and constitutional foundation on which America was built – a foundation which, in recent years, has been seriously attacked and undermined,” the organization's website reads.

Politicians, like Rep. Nate Schatzline, have joined forces with this movement, which has taken form during election seasons as a hydralike network of political action committees and mega-donors. The relentless network has secured stronghold majorities on Texas school boards in seven districts since 2021. Of the 42 candidates financially backed by Texans for Educational Freedom, a major force in this effort, 76% won their races. The organization boasts an “88% win rate in 2022.”

Often these PAC-backed candidates have made their first standing in local activism, advocating to ban LGBTQ+ and BIPOC books from school libraries. With local school district policy at the helm of this vocal minority, school boards have created divisions in juxtaposition to their district administration, ignoring student outcomes in favor of scoring political points.

Because they were elected on platforms to ban books, it's not surprising for some new school boards to hastily adopt controversial policies in their first actions on the dais. The Conroe ISD board voted in November to give themselves the power to remove books from school libraries, regardless of considerations made by librarians, administrators, and even community review committees established at the urgent call to give parents a voice. Ruptures to relationships between school districts and their stakeholders, alongside the chilling effect of state policy like the H.B. 900 book ban, have led to a swift increase in censorship, specifically of books simply about LGBTQ+ topics.

The issue censors have with books is evidently not with harmful content but with identity. Book bans were merely another medium in which political efforts have targeted vulnerable students.

Amid increasing anti-transgender rhetoric in policymaking over the years, the Texas Legislature proposed 141 anti-LGBTQ+ bills this year alone, nearly double that of 2021 and more than nine times that of 2019. Two bills that passed include criminalizing gender-affirming care for minors and drag performances in the presence of minors, currently blocked by a federal judge.

Roughly half of the bills proposed during this year's session relate to education censorship, unlike the previous four sessions which had nearly zero related propositions, aside from sports bans against transgender students.

Following suit with state trends in Katy ISD, the school board adopted a comprehensive “Parental Rights & Gender Fluidity” policy in August. The board's decision came within one month of expanding its direct power over library books, seeking to remove “Pride sections” from school libraries.

The policy, which specifically targets transgender students, requires district personnel to inform a student's parent if they seek guidance or support for their gender identity. LGBTQ+ student clubs, classroom discussions, library books, and any other use of district funds to promote “gender fluidity” are strictly prohibited by the policy, along with bathroom and sports bans against transgender students.

The policy's main objectives are outlined in the text overview as follows:

  1. Biological females and males and sex-specific spaces are safeguarded;
  2. District facilities such as bathrooms, locker rooms, and changing facilities are separated by Biological Sex;
  3. the pronouns used for persons on campus are consistent with the Biological Sex of the person;
  4. Gender Fluidity content is excluded from the classroom and instructional materials; and
  5. District staff will not diagnose or treat gender dysphoria and will respect the right of the parent to determine what is best for the welfare of their child.
  6. The District's ultimate goal is to ensure students are safe to learn and grow, and not to engage in the social transitioning of students.
Katy ISD Gender Fluidity Policy - DRAFT - 8.16.2023 - CLEAN.pdf

After more than four hours of public comments at the board meeting and about 400 community members showing up to oppose the policy, the board cast a 4-3 vote, nearing midnight, along PAC-backed party lines. In the eight weeks following, the district outed 19 students to their families, averaging a notification incident roughly every three days.

In response to the policy, which the ACLU of Texas says violates the state's ethics codes, youth-led nonprofit Students Engaged in Advancing Texas (SEAT) filed a federal civil rights complaint with the U.S. Department of Education. The advocacy group seeks a complete repeal of the policy, direct resources to support impacted students, LGBTQ+ inclusion training for district personnel, and the inclusion of LGBTQ+ protections in the district's nondiscrimination statement.

"This policy tries to endanger and erase trans students all under the misleading guise of parental authority and student safety, when it is clearly driven by ignorance and hate," said Jarred Burton, Katy ISD junior and president of their school's Sexuality and Gender Alliance. Burton has been actively mobilizing their peers to speak out against the policy at board meetings and in local press. "Attempts at mental health awareness mean nothing with policies like this in place attempting to erase queer youth."

This resounding opposition is not uncommon in Texas, a state with a very youthful population, predominantly composed of people of color. Furthermore, Generation Z is the most openly queer generation in history, with 17% of young adults under 30 identifying as lesbian, gay, or bisexual, and 5% as transgender or non-binary. Queer resilience was exemplified in Katy's inaugural pride festival bringing together more than 2,000 residents.

While marginalized communities are quite visible in the Texas public sphere, their political prowess often remains belittled because their representation in policymaking remains disproportional. The Voting Rights Act of 1965 was another victory for leaders of the Civil Rights Movement, breaking barriers for voters of color to participate in the United States' democratic institutions. However, leaders in Texas often tried their best to circumvent this federal legislation as they did with the Supreme Court's decision in Brown.

Still today, many school districts in Texas violate Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act with “at-large” elections. Rather than each local community electing a school board member to represent their designated area, as is the case with single-member districts in Congress, many school boards are elected to positions by the entire district's electorate. Though a member's term could last for a few years, select positions are on the ballot each year, all to be decided by relatively identical electorates. For majority-minority school districts, this system empowers a wealthier – and evidently Whiter – demographic because of voter turnout patterns and candidacy stemming more frequently from wealthier areas.

Before casting doubt on why democratic elections disproportionately benefit a particular demographic, the answer is clear from centuries of voter suppression. More recently, directly preceding the concentrated movement on the frontlines of Texas education, the state legislature passed a “voter suppression bill” in September 2021 that specifically targeted initiatives by officials in majority-minority Harris County to streamline voting for often disenfranchised voters. Subsequently, the legislature intensified gerrymandering in their decennial redistricting process. Now, 70% of the Texas Senate is White while only 40% of the state population is White, according to census definitions.

With an inaccurate representation of Texas in the state's policymaking, there's no obligation for political leaders to serve the interests of Texans, much less the state's youngest generation. However, amid the legislature's crusade to assert more authority over public education, the Texas House passed H.B. 2647. Developed by 8th grade student in Round Rock ISD, Ayaan Moledina, the bill would allow school districts to appoint a non-voting student trustee to serve on its school board. The Texas Senate, however, did not take up the proposition before its deadline, leaving the bill to die. Nevertheless, it was a short-lived glimpse into what is possible from students involved in policymaking.

Texas House and Senate seats are up for reelection in November 2024, and several moderate conservatives – like Rep. Andrew Murr who received 80% of the general vote in 2022 – are not seeking a renewal of their term. Others are facing primary challengers backed by Gov. Abbott per his promise to challenge any Republican who took a stand against his interest in vouchers, which did not deter some rural Republicans from remaining steadfast in their opposition. Even Democratic voucher opponents like Rep. Abel Herrero, who has served for nearly two decades, will not seek reelection.

The future of voucher legislation is unclear, but mounting efforts by lawmakers and interest groups to seize control of Texas education is at the forefront of the state's political prospect. Examining key players, movement tactics, and desired outcomes reveal clear trends about the various social movements in Texas and how everyday Texans have little voice in the decisions directly impacting the public. The outlook for Texas education favors the new fashion of growing conservatism before the state will embolden marginalized voices in public education.

Congruence exists among the modern movement to censor, the “school choice” and “parental rights” movements, and continued efforts to deny equal opportunity and respect in societal deference from White supremacy, Christian nationalism, patriarchy, and heteronormativity. For as long as the finance and fear that fuels extremism outweighs the call for a moral revival, students' only voice in decisions affecting their futures may rely on their privilege.
